# Delivery Systems at Scale

> **Source**: Reforge - Scaling Product Delivery Program
> **Authors**: Andy Johns (Partner, Unusual Ventures) & Matt Greenberg (CTO, Reforge)

---

## Executive Summary

Scaling product delivery requires moving beyond manual processes to systematic approaches. Companies that succeed build "pulley systems" - structured frameworks that make delivering innovative products reliable and repeatable, rather than dependent on heroic individual efforts.

---

## The Core Problem

### The Boulder Metaphor

Product development is like lifting boulders:

| Stage | Boulder Size | System Required |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| Pre-PMF | Small | Manual, all-hands effort works |
| Post-PMF Growth | Medium | Quick wins, small features |
| Scale | Large | Big-bet innovations require new systems |

**Why manual lifting stops working at scale:**
- Team grows larger, coordination becomes harder
- Product and tech stack become more complex
- Growth requires step-change innovations, not just iterations

### Why Big Bets Matter

1. **Customer expectations evolve** - Users expect increasing value over time; only big bets deliver step-change improvements
2. **Growth velocity requirements** - Most companies need 2-3x YoY growth; small iterations can't sustain this rate

## Three Company Archetypes That Struggle

### 1. The Hacker Company

**Behavior**: Avoids big boulders; scales through many "quick win" products

**Why it fails**:
- Hits diminishing returns on quick-win releases
- Growth stagnates without fundamental product evolution
- Short development cycles don't require complex processes

### 2. The Fixer Company

**Behavior**: Relies on rockstar individuals who "patch together" processes to deliver

**Why it fails**:
- Doesn't scale - rockstars burn out
- Creates bottleneck around one person's bandwidth
- Limited by ability to recruit new rockstars

### 3. The Visionary Company

**Behavior**: Consistently generates great ideas but fails to execute

**Why it fails**:
- Expects teams to "figure out" execution without systems
- Visibility goes to ideation, not delivery
- Credibility erodes when products fail to ship or flop

> "Your best idea that never makes it to market means nothing to your customer, to your company, and ultimately to your career." — Matt Greenberg

## Symptoms vs. Outcomes

### Typical Companies Experience:
- Frequent late-stage blow-ups in major development processes
- Products delivered behind schedule and over budget
- Poor or inconsistent tradeoff decisions
- Customers underwhelmed by new releases

### Great Companies Achieve:
- Strategic alignment established early and frequently re-aligned
- Critical launch timelines met within budget constraints
- Stated strategy for making tradeoff decisions
- Consistently delighted customers

## The Four-Pulley System

The solution is a systematic approach with four key elements:

### 1. Product Conviction
**Purpose**: Build confidence that you're solving the right problem

**Problem it solves**: Big-bet innovations often fail because they're driven by a single leader's opinion rather than validated understanding of customer value.

**Mechanism**: Introduce processes to validate Product Intuition and dramatically reduce failure likelihood.

### 2. Dynamic Delivery Plan
**Purpose**: Balance structure and flexibility in planning

**Problem it solves**: Standard methodologies (Agile, Waterfall) fail to handle the uncertainty of large-scale innovations.

**Mechanism**: Hybrid methodology that evolves over time while minimizing risk and yield loss.

### 3. Execution & Adaptation
**Purpose**: Implement plans while maintaining alignment through new learnings

**Problem it solves**: Product reviews become status updates rather than tools for driving development forward.

**Mechanism**: Transform reviews into powerful feedback mechanisms that inform decisions.

### 4. Launch Strategy
**Purpose**: Maximize impact after development completes

**Problem it solves**: Companies end their strategy at launch rather than planning for impact delivery.

**Mechanism**: Structure launch plans that assess readiness, maximize value, and manage tech debt.

## Critical Success Factor: Product-Engineering Alignment

> "Most PMs feel like they wish they knew more about engineering; most engineers incorrectly feel like they know everything a PM knows." — Matt Greenberg

### Alignment Failures and Consequences

| Misalignment Area | Result |
|-------------------|--------|
| Customer problem definition | Can't build conviction in potential impact |
| Solution design | Best delivery plan falls apart during build |

### Building "Superpower" Elements

Best companies identify specific steps where they differentiate their product development:

| Element | Company Example |
|---------|-----------------|
| Building Conviction | Google (Design Sprint process) |
| Delivery Planning | Asana |
| Product Reviews | Intuit, Amazon, Wealthfront |
| Product Launch | Credit Karma |
